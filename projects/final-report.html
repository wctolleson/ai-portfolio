Final Report

Artificial Intelligence Adoption in Construction Management

Research Overview

Full title: Artificial Intelligence Adoption in Construction Management: A Multi-Stakeholder Study of Contractors, Owners, and Consultants

This final report examines how AI is being adopted in construction management and aviation-infrastructure settings, what barriers are slowing implementation, and what practices make adoption more likely to create measurable value.

The research combines a literature review with semi-structured interviews representing consultant, owner/operator, business intelligence, and general contractor perspectives from TYLin, Charlotte Douglas International Airport, and JE Dunn Construction.

Research Question

How are construction and aviation-infrastructure organizations adopting AI, what barriers are slowing implementation, and what practices make adoption more likely to create measurable value?

Key Findings

1. Adoption is active but immature

Organizations are moving beyond curiosity, but broad enterprise integration is still evolving.

2. Practical workflows lead

The strongest current AI use cases are narrow tasks such as reporting, document review, schedule checks, quality planning, and meeting summaries.

3. Data readiness controls value

Clean, governed, context-rich data is the foundation for reliable AI outputs.

4. Governance determines scale

Security, vendor transparency, approved platforms, and human oversight decide which tools can move beyond pilots.

5. AI augments people

The strongest pattern is human-in-the-loop decision support, not autonomous replacement of construction professionals.

6. ROI must include hidden labor

Training, integration, data cleanup, governance, and ongoing administration must be counted alongside license costs.

Recommendations

  • Start with real workflow problems, not tools. Early use cases should be narrow, measurable, and low-risk.
  • Build data governance before expecting transformation. AI quality depends on reliable data and business context.
  • Use approved enterprise platforms where possible. Construction and aviation data require clear security boundaries.
  • Keep humans in the loop. AI may support cost, schedule, safety, quality, and contract review, but professionals remain accountable.
  • Develop power users and champions. Adoption spreads through practical examples from people who understand the work.
  • Measure practical value. Track time saved, reduced rework, better documentation, faster decisions, and risk reduction.

Final Takeaway

AI adoption in construction management is no longer theoretical, but the best near-term value comes from disciplined, human-centered workflow augmentation. AI will not build buildings by itself, but it can help prepared PM/CM teams review faster, document better, forecast earlier, and advise owners more effectively.
