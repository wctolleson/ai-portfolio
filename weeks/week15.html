Week 15: Higher Education and AI

Colleges Ramp Up Offerings to Teach Students to Be AI Ethicists

Article Overview

This Higher Ed Dive article reports that colleges are expanding courses, certificates, and degree pathways in AI ethics because employers increasingly need graduates who can evaluate whether AI systems are biased, unsafe, privacy-threatening, noncompliant, or simply wrong.

Source: Higher Ed Dive — "Colleges ramp up offerings to teach students to be AI ethicists"

Published: April 7, 2026

Main Findings

The article argues that colleges are expanding AI ethics programs because employers increasingly need graduates who can critically evaluate AI systems. Higher Ed Dive presents AI ethics as an interdisciplinary field rather than a purely technical one, noting that many programs are designed for students without deep computer science backgrounds.

Key findings include:

  • A 2025 Lightcast report showed ninefold growth in nontechnical job postings requiring generative AI skills from 2022 to 2024
  • More than 100,000 jobs per year now seek AI-ethics expertise
  • AI ethicists are expected to ensure an organization's AI use aligns with law, internal codes of conduct, and trustworthiness standards
  • Students are asked to evaluate real-world problems: hallucinations, emergency shutoff protocols, regulatory compliance, and consequences of poorly tested AI products

The underlying argument is that ethical AI education is not abstract — it addresses tangible harms from chatbots that fabricate facts and legal research tools that cite nonexistent cases.

Broader Implications for Society

The broader implication is that higher education is moving from teaching students merely how to use AI toward teaching them how to govern it responsibly.

Ethical AI literacy is becoming a society-wide need because AI now affects:

  • Business and healthcare decisions
  • Legal and government processes
  • Everyday decision-making across industries

The article frames ethics as a safeguard against social harm by emphasizing bias, privacy, faulty outputs, misleading language, and the risk of releasing human-like AI systems without adequate testing or oversight.

A key insight: regulation and ethical reasoning are complements, not opposites. Organizations should ask whether AI deployments produce genuinely positive social outcomes rather than only commercial gains.

Relevance to the Construction Industry

For construction, the article is highly relevant because the same ethical issues apply to AI uses in project controls, scheduling, estimating, safety monitoring, document review, procurement, and design support.

By inference, the article suggests that construction firms will need personnel who can:

  • Question data quality and identify bias
  • Verify outputs before they affect decisions
  • Protect privacy and sensitive project information
  • Ensure human oversight remains in place before AI-generated recommendations affect cost, schedule, safety, or claims decisions

This has implications for construction education as well. Programs in construction management and engineering may increasingly need to teach students not just how to apply AI, but how to recognize when a model is unreliable, when a dataset is incomplete, and when legal or ethical constraints should limit automation.

Future construction professionals will need both operational AI literacy and governance skills.

Source Verification
