Week 11: Societal AI Impact

Fed Races to Adapt to AI Promises and Pitfalls for Jobs, Inflation

Article Overview

This Reuters article examines how the U.S. Federal Reserve is grappling with artificial intelligence as a major economic force, with policymakers divided over how AI will affect employment and inflation. The article presents competing views on whether AI will be disinflationary or could create new challenges for monetary policy.

Main Findings

The article presents two competing views within the Federal Reserve:

View 1 (Kevin Warsh): AI could become a disinflationary force by raising productivity and supporting lower interest rates.

View 2 (Current Fed consensus): AI may increase structural unemployment while not reducing inflation enough to justify easier monetary policy.

Key evidence cited includes:

  • Block's announcement of roughly 4,000 AI-related layoffs as an example of how quickly AI may reshape white-collar work
  • Fed officials' concern that displaced workers may take longer to find new jobs
  • AI-related capital spending, higher returns to capital, and pressure on electricity and building costs could keep inflation elevated

Fed Governor Lisa Cook warned that AI-driven unemployment may not signal normal economic slack, and traditional rate cuts could increase inflationary pressure instead of solving the problem.

Broader Implications for Society

The broader societal implication is that AI may weaken the traditional assumption that higher unemployment automatically leads to lower inflation and easier policy responses.

Society could face a period in which:

  • Some workers lose bargaining power or jobs while overall output and investment remain strong
  • Gains from AI flow more heavily to capital owners, highly skilled workers, and firms able to deploy AI effectively
  • Displaced workers face longer adjustment periods

Fed Vice Chair Michael Barr warned that widespread AI-driven displacement could create major social and distributional problems and require changes to:

  • The social safety net
  • Education and training systems
  • Workforce development programs

The article suggests that AI is not simply a technology story—it is a labor-market, inequality, and public-policy issue with consequences for households, wages, and economic stability.

Relevance to the Construction Industry

For the construction industry, the article is relevant in three key ways:

1. White-Collar Impact: AI may affect not only field labor but also technical roles. Reuters notes AI's growing ability to perform tasks such as coding and data analysis. This has implications for preconstruction, estimating, scheduling, BIM coordination, design support, and project controls—where AI may raise productivity while also changing staffing needs.

2. Infrastructure Demand: AI buildout is already straining electricity and building costs in some areas. Demand for data centers, power infrastructure, and related facilities can increase opportunities for contractors while also intensifying cost pressure on labor, materials, and energy-intensive projects.

3. Financing Conditions: If the Fed concludes that AI-related unemployment does not justify rapid rate cuts, financing conditions could remain tighter for longer. This matters for construction because interest rates influence private development, capital project timing, and owner willingness to move projects forward.
