Week 10: Vibe Coding

5 Vibe Coding Use Cases Every Company Can Start Using Today

Article Overview

This Forbes article by Bernard Marr argues that vibe coding — using AI to turn plain language descriptions into lightweight, functional tools — is removing one of the most persistent bottlenecks in business: the gap between what teams need and what engineering capacity can deliver.

1. Main Findings and Arguments

Vibe Coding Democratizes Software Creation

By allowing people closest to the work to build their own lightweight solutions, organizations can test ideas faster, reduce friction in internal processes, and learn what actually delivers value — without waiting in a long development queue.

Five Practical Use Cases for Immediate Adoption

  1. Prototyping and Innovation: Vibe-coded prototypes can quickly communicate a product's value proposition with minimal R&D spend, allowing teams to test new features or concepts before committing significant resources.
  2. Automating Internal Workflows: Lightweight tools can eliminate email chains and manual handoffs for processes like employee onboarding, purchase order approvals, and marketing content scheduling.
  3. Sales and Customer Support: Custom virtual assistants and AI agents can be tailored to specific customer challenges, such as guiding sales staff through objections or helping support teams resolve frequent errors.
  4. Reporting and Dashboarding: Teams can quickly build natural language-native tools that answer specific business questions, presenting insights in plain language rather than complex charts.
  5. Compliance and Regulatory Checks: With appropriate human oversight, vibe coding can generate checklists, flag missing approvals, monitor for legislative changes, and prepare audit documentation.

Clear Limits Are Acknowledged

Mission-critical systems, customer-facing products, and anything involving sensitive data still require professional engineering, strong governance, and security by design. Vibe coding is positioned as a tool for internal and low-risk use cases, not a wholesale replacement for software development.

2. Broader Implications for Society

A Cultural Shift

Marr argues that the ability to experiment by building bespoke tools will become a core professional capability across functions — not just within engineering or IT teams. This has implications for how organizations hire, train, and structure their workforces.

Innovation Speed as Competitive Advantage

Businesses that embed vibe coding into their culture will be able to act while others are still planning — testing ideas in the real world rather than waiting for them to be perfected on the drawing board. Over time, this compounds into a significant strategic advantage.

Power Shifts to Domain Experts

When the people closest to a problem can build their own solutions, institutional knowledge becomes more actionable. This could gradually reduce organizations' dependence on centralized IT functions for routine internal tooling.

Governance Remains Essential

Speed and accessibility do not eliminate the need for professional discipline — particularly in compliance, data protection, and customer-facing contexts.

3. Relevance to the Construction Industry

While written for a general business audience, the implications for construction are significant and direct:

a. Internal Workflows Are an Immediate Opportunity

Construction firms are heavily burdened by manual administrative processes — daily logs, RFI tracking, submittal lists, progress reporting, and punch lists. Vibe coding offers a practical path to automating these workflows without expensive custom software development, particularly for mid-size and smaller firms that lack dedicated engineering resources.

b. Custom Dashboards for Project Data

Construction projects generate vast amounts of data that rarely surfaces in a usable form. Vibe-coded reporting tools could allow project teams to ask plain language questions of their own data — costs to date, schedule variances, open issues — without navigating complex BI platforms.

c. Compliance and Safety Documentation

Construction is one of the most compliance-intensive industries, with OSHA requirements, building codes, submittal approvals, and contract obligations creating constant administrative pressure. Vibe-coded compliance tools — with appropriate human oversight — could:

  • Automate checklist generation
  • Flag missing sign-offs
  • Monitor for regulatory changes in real time

d. Prototyping Client-Facing Tools

For construction technology teams and innovation-minded firms, vibe coding lowers the barrier to testing new ideas with clients or project teams — whether that's a custom progress dashboard, a field reporting interface, or a cost tracking tool specific to a project type.

Key Takeaway

Marr's article makes a compelling case that vibe coding is less a software trend and more a strategic posture — one that rewards organizations willing to experiment quickly and build a culture of continuous iteration. For construction firms navigating both a labor shortage and a technology adoption gap, that posture may prove particularly valuable.
